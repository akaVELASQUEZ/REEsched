Load Schedule

Load schedule is a summary of the data of each circuits in an electrical system. It holds the information about how much current a circuit draws, the rating of the equipments or appliances and how many circuits the system have. It also contains information about the circuit breaker and the wire size that should be used that would help electricians in their installation. It could also help so that you'll know if you could still add more load in your circuits or panels for your future expansions.

Another reason to make load schedule is the fact that it's a requirement in the electrical plan for the building permit, occupancy permit, maintenance and electrical distribution companies/cooperatives.



Step 1


To be able to use this web app, you need information about your electrical layout. 1st step is to secure yourself an electrical layout of your residence. Electrical layout plans is usually divided into 2, a lighting layout that contains the lightings in your residence and how the lightings are connected. Another one is power layout where convenience outlets and appliances are located.


Step 2

Once you secured yourself an electrical plan, you now need to get the data that you'll input in the table. First is the lighting layout. In the example, you'll see the lightings in the plan as well as their connection. At the end of the connections, there's a circle with LP 8 or LP 9 writen, which indicates the end of the circuit. You'll need to count the number of lightings connected in each of the circuits of the system. In the example, both LP8 and LP9 contains 11 lightings.


Step 3

After the lighting layout, go to the power layout. Same with that you did in step 2, count the number on convenience outlet in the circuit till you reach the end or the circle. Sometimes, specially when dealing with residential plans, power layouts also contains things kitchen appliances and many other appliances that usually needs separate circuit breakers.


Step 4

On the power layout, take note of the circuit numbers of each appliances on the plan. Then look at the rating of the appliance that you have or plan to use and use those ratings on the table. Most of the time each appliance requires a separate circuit breaker to ensure the protection of the equipment and the system and to avoid overloading the circuit that might result to electrical accidents.
